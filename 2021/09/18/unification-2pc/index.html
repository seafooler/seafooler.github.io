Unification of Two-phase Consensus

Posted on 2021-09-18 Edited on 2021-12-04 In Consensus

In the last two blogs, we derivated the design of two-phase BFT consensuses. We picked two different routines, namely PBFT and Tendermint. In this blog, we try to carry out a unification of two-phase consensus. Based on the unification model, we will clarify how PBFT/Tendermint implements the model in the upcoming blogs.

I. Three Ingredients

Almost all the two-phase consensus consists of three ingredients:

  • 1st-phase vote (1PV)
  • 2nd-phase vote (2PV)
  • View change process (VC)

1PV corresponds to prepare phase in PBFT or prevote phase in Tendermint; 2PV corresponds to commit phase in PBFT or precommit phase in Tendermint; VC corresponds to view change in PBFT or round change in Tendermint.

II. Necessity of Three Ingredients

It is easy to understand that one-phase vote is necessary for a BFT consensus.

To rescue the system from a halting status (faulty leader), VC is also necessary.

However, the single-phase vote cannot assure safety across views. To achieve across-view safety, an extra phase of vote is needed. The reason why an extra phase of vote can do it will be presented below.

Note that the newly-added phase of vote is considered as 1PV in Section I, while the preexisting phase of vote is considered as 2PV in Section I. The reason is that 2PV specifies the time to commit a request, which matches the specification of the preexisting phase of vote.

III. How to Achieve Safety Without Compromising Liveness?

For the sake of presentation, we borrow a notion of polka from Tendermint. The 2/3+ votes in 1PV constitutes a polka.

As already talked about in the PBFT blog, the most challenge task to achieve across-view safety is to judge if a request \(r\) has been committed in the old view. Besides, designing the judgement method cannot compromise liveness. Based on the judgement, a replica decices if it should vote for a new request or not:

  • If a replica judges \(r\) has been committed before in the index \(i\), it will decline to vote for any other \(r'\) (\(r' \neq r\));
  • Otherwise, it will vote for any \(r'\).

For a replica to make a judgement, a natural manner is to communicate with other replicas and collect others' opinions. In the remaining parts, we will talk about how to express an opinion and how to make a judgement.

A. Formats of opinion expression

The expression of opinions can be of two formats naturally:

  • Polka/empty (PE in short): a replica broadcasts polka if there is one, otherwise empty;
  • True/false (TF in short): a replica broadcasts true if there is a polka, othwewise false;

B. PE format

The PE format is usually assisted with the following judgement rule:

  1. If a replica receives any polka for \(r\), it will judge \(r\) as committed before.
  2. Otherwise, a replica will judge \(r\) as uncommitted before.

The PE format can deduce deterministic, partly-correct, and partly-consistent judgement results in all the correct replicas. To be more specific, when a replica collects 2/3+ opinions for a request \(r\), we can get the following deduction: \[ C(r)=1 \Longrightarrow \forall \space rep_j \space \exists \space polka_r \in O_j \] where \(C\) denotes whether \(r\) has been committed before, while \(O_j\) represents the set of opinions collected by replica \(rep_j\).

Namely, if \(r\) has been committed before, each (correct) replica must collect a opinion of polka on \(r\) and makes the judgement as committed. In this case, all the replicas can make correct and consistent judgements.

By contrast, if \(r\) has not been committed before, some replicas may collect a opinion of polka on \(r\) and make the judgement as committed, while others may collect all the opinions of empty and make the judgement as uncommitted. In this case, replicas may make inconsistent judgements and some of them may be wrong. We refer to the wrong result (mistaking uncommitted as committed) as False Committed (FC), as an analogy with false positive in machine learning fields.

To sum up, PE format and direct broadcasting of opinions will lead to partly-correct and partly-consistent judgement results.

  • Partly-correct: Note that FC results (mistaking an uncommitted request as committed) are acceptable, which will not violate the safety property.
  • Partly-consistent: Inconsistent results are acceptable too. When the replica with committed result becomes the leader, it will repropose \(r\), which will get votes from all the replicas and finally be committed.

Besides, the judgement rule will not lead the system to halt, which guarantee the liveness.

An optimization to PE: consistent judgement results

Although the PE format and the assistant judgement rule above are enough to make a correct judgement, it may be inefficient. Particularly, when inconsistent results are gotten by different replicas, the leaders may be switched many times until the replica with committed result becomes the leader.

On the contrary, if all the replicas can get (totally) consistent judgement results, either totally FC or totally correct, the new request proposed by a new leader can be committed soon.

To get the (totally) consistent judgement results, we can carry out an optimization as follows:

  • Replicas send their opinions to the new leader instead of a broadcast. The new leader packages and broadcasts the collected 2/3+ opinions.

Actually, this optimization is introduced by PBFT, where the opinion is named view change message and the package of opinions is named new view message. From this point, PBFT can be classified as more a three-phase consensus than a two-phase one, since view change is also a phase of message broadcasting.

With this optimization, PBFT can commit new requests at the pace of network delay, thus achieving responsiveness.

C. TF format

First of all, the TF format seems to bring lower communication complexity (tiny size of true/false messages) than PE format. However, it also bring some troubles. Following the judgement rule of PE format, the TF format can be assisted with two different judgement rules (rules 1 & 2). However, as we will see below, both rule 1 and rule 2 cannot work well. Therefore, more variants of rules are proposed.

1. Rule 1: based on One true （no safety or no liveness）

The first judgement rule is quite similar to that in PE format:

  1. If a replica receives any true for \(r\), it will judge \(r\) as committed before.
  2. Otherwise, a replica will judge \(r\) as uncommitted before.

A similar deduction can be expressed as follows: \[ C(r)=1 \Longrightarrow \forall \space rep_j \space \exists \space true_r \in O_j \]

It seems that we can next apply the analysis for PE format in Section I.C.2 to here too. Unfortunately, we cannot! The most essential reason is that an opinion of TF format can be falstified easily. To be more specific, although an committed request \(r\) must correspond to a true in \(O_j\), a byzantine replica can easily falstify and broadcast a opinion of true for \(r'\). A replica with two true opinions for contradictory requests (\(r\) and \(r'\)) cannot distinguish which one was exactly committed. If a replica elects \(r\) or \(r'\) casually, the system may get a wrong result, thus violating safety. On the contrary, if a replica declines to elect either one, the system may get halted, thus compromising liveness.

By contrast, with the PE format, there would never be two polkas for contradictory requests.

In conclusion, TF format plus rule 1 cannot work well.

2. Rule 2: based on 2/3+ true (no safety)

The second judgement rule is more conservative:

  1. If a replica receives 2/3+ true for \(r\), it will judge \(r\) as committed before.
  2. Otherwise, a replica will judge \(r\) as uncommitted before.

First of all, different from rule 1, rule 2 can guarantee that:

  • A correct replica will judge at most one request as committed;
  • If two correct replicas judge \(r\) and \(r'\) as committed respectively, \(r\) must equal \(r'\).

However, collection of 2/3+ true for \(r\) can be non-deterministic, even if \(r\) has been committed before. In other words, a committed request may be mistaken as uncommitted in the new view, which violates safety. In fact, this problem can be named as false uncommitted (FU), which mistakes a committed request as uncommitted. FU is unacceptable.

3. A remedy to rule 2 (Rule 3): Add a new phase

Exactly, we do not need all the correct replicas to judge the committed request (\(r\)) as committed. As long as there are 1/3+ correct replicas do it, there cannot be enough votes for \(r'\), thus guaranteeing safety.

To achieve this, a new phase is added to the normal case, which transforms the two-phase consensus (2PC in short) to a three-phase one (3PC in short), as follows:

  • 1st-phase vote (1PV)
  • 2nd-phase vote (2PV)
  • 3rd-phase vote (3PV)
  • View change process (VC)

Note that 3PV in 3PC corresponds to 2PV in 2PC, and 1PV in 3PC corresponds to 1PV in 2PC. 2PV in 3PC corresponds to the judgement process. The core design of 3PC can be described as folllows:

  • 1PV in 3PC works in the same way as that in 2PC;
  • In 2PV, a replica will broadcast true opinion if it possesses a polka; otherwise, broadcast false;
  • If a replica receives 2/3+ true in 2PV, it will broadcast true opinion in 3PV; otherwise, broadcast false;
  • Only 2/3+ votes are collected in 3PV, will the request \(r\) be committed.

For the sake of presentation, we denote the 2/3+ true votes collected in 2PV as kalpo. Therefore, a replica with kalpo will judge \(r\) as committed.

Next, we talk about how 3PC achieves safety and liveness respectively.

  • Safety: If \(r\) is committed, at least 1/3+ correct replicas possess a kalpo. These replicas will not vote for another \(r'\), thus achieving safety.
  • Liveness: (1) If no correct replica possesses kalpo, which means each correct replica judges \(r\) as uncommitted, all the correct replicas will vote for a new request \(r'\). Thus, \(r'\) can be committed finally and liveness is achieved. (2) If at least one correct replica possesses kalpo, when it becomes the leader, it will repropose \(r\). \(r\) will be voted by all the correct replicas and be committed. Therefore, liveness can also be achieved.
An optimization to rule 3

As stated in the discussion of liveness, if one correct replica (\(p\)) possesses kalpo, the system will not commit a new request, until the \(p\) becomes the leader. This can be inefficient.

To accelerate the consensus, an optimization is added to rule 3:

  • In VC, each replica will broadcast its polka/empty to the new leader;
  • If the new leader receives a polka for \(r\), it will propose \(r\); otherwise, it can propose an arbitrary request.

As stated before, if any correct replica possesses a kalpo for \(r\), at least 1/3+ correct replicas possess a polka for \(r\). With this optimization, the new leader can definitely receive a polka from the 1/3+ replicas. Therefore, the new leader can definitely propose \(r\) voted by all the correct replicas.

Exacty, Hotstuff elects this routine: TF format + rule 3 + optimization.

4. Rule 4: based on 2/3+ false (no liveness)

Since collecting 2/3+ true opinions in rule 2 may violate safety, what if we choose the opposite side:

  1. If a replica receives 2/3+ false for \(r\), it will judge \(r\) as uncommitted before.
  2. Otherwise, it will judge \(r\) as committed before.

On the other hand, we can get the deduction as follows: \[ C(r)=1 \Longrightarrow \forall \space rep_j \space |\{o | o \in O_j \& o = false\}|<2/3 \] This deduction plus rule 4 can guarantee safety. The reason is that if \(r\) was committed before, none of replicas can collect 2/3+ false opinions, thus judging \(r\) as uncommitted. On the other hand, if \(r\) was not committed before, some replicas may judge it as uncommitted while others judge it as committed. In this regard, there will also be a situation of false committed (FC). Fortunately, FC will not violate safety property, whose reason has been presented in Section III.B.

However, rule 4 cannot guarantee liveness. Consider the example as follows:

  • A byzantine replica arbitrarily broadcasts true opinion for the uncommitted \(r\).
  • Each correct replica makes the judgement of \(r\) as committed. However, \(r\) received by the correct replicas are totally different. When a correct replica becomes the leader and propose a request, the request will be rejected by all the other correct replicas.
  • When byzantine replicas become leaders, they do not propose any request.
  • The system halts.

5. A remedy to rule 4 (rule 5)

On the basis of rule 4, we can futher ask any replica broadcasting true to attach polka. polka can prevent a Byzantine replica from broadcasting an arbitrary request. Therefore, if two correct replicas judge two requests as committed, these requests must be the same.

6. Another remedy to rule 4 (rule 6)

The liveness problem in Section III.C.4 occurs when a Byzantine replica sends different arbitrary \(r_j\) to different replicas (\(rep_j\)) and \(rep_j\) happens to receive \(r_j\) as its 2/3 opinions. In this case, each correct replica \(rep_j\) mistakes \(r_j\) as committed and will not for any other request.

However, if each replica does not make any judgement as committed according to the collection of TF opinions, judgement result will not be polluted by Byzantine replicas and liveness can be achieved. Therefore, another remedy to rule 4 (rule 6) is as follows:

  1. If a replica has a polka for \(r\), it will judge \(r\) as committed before;
  2. If a replica receives 2/3+ false for \(r\), it will judge \(r\) as uncommitted before;
  3. Otherwise, just keep waiting.

This rule can guarantee that if multiple replicas make judgements as committed for requests, all the requests are equal to each other. With this rule, let us discuss two cases:

  • Case 1 (At least a correct replica possesses a polka for \(r\)): When one of these replicas becomes the new leader, it will propose \(r\). All the replicas (either judge it as committed or uncommitted) will vote for it. \(r\) will finally be committed by all the replicas.
  • Case 2 (No correct relica possesses a polka for \(r\)): Each correct replica will definitely receive 2/3+ false from all the correct replicas and judges \(r\) as uncommitted. In this regard, no matter a new leader propose any new request \(r'\) (\(r'\) may equal \(r\)), each correct replica will vote for it. \(r'\) will finally be committed by all the replicas.

To sum up, liveness property can be achieved.

Actually, Tendermint elects this routine: TF format plus rule 6.
